Features as Statistics, Not Atoms

A useful starting point is to treat features as statistics that a model extracts from data. Some extracted quantities are useful, some are weakly related to the task, and some are simply noise from the perspective of the loss.

In a randomly initialized model, we should not expect these extracted quantities to carry stable meaning. They are not yet aligned with the task. But once training starts under a good objective, the model is pressured to discover statistics that reduce loss. At that point, some of these statistics become meaningful features in a functional sense: they support better predictions.

Core claim. A feature is meaningful because of what it does for optimization and behavior, not because it is semantically clean in isolation.

Why linear features often appear

Many studies suggest that useful features are often linear in some sense. One plausible reason is architectural: transformers rely heavily on linear maps, dot products, and additive updates in the residual stream. If internal computation is built from many linear pieces, linear readouts of structure are expected to be common.

But this should not be overgeneralized. A sublayer, or a composition of sublayers, is still a powerful function that can transform extracted features into highly context-dependent outputs. From this viewpoint, linearity may be an effective approximation in many regimes without being the full ontology of representation.

\[ x^{(\ell+1)} = x^{(\ell)} + F_\ell\!\left(x^{(\ell)}\right) \] \[ \text{Linear structure is common, but } F_\ell \text{ can still induce nonlinear behavior in composition.} \]

Limits of the Linear Representation Hypothesis

Evidence from sparse autoencoders (SAEs) suggests caution. In some settings, reconstruction error remains large, implying that important information may not be captured by a small set of isolated linear directions.

This leaves at least three possibilities:

  1. Residual information. Important signal may live in reconstruction error rather than in decoded sparse features.
  2. Nonlinear structure. Relevant computation may depend on curved manifolds or nonlinear interactions.
  3. Relational encoding. Meaning may live in relationships among features, not in any single feature alone.

The limited effectiveness of steering with one vector direction supports this concern. If a concept is distributed across many related features, nudging one direction may only weakly perturb the underlying computation.

The many-pipes view of concepts

For large models, a concept should not be expected to correspond to one unique feature. A better picture is a large family of related features carrying overlapping pieces of the same concept.

A helpful analogy is many pipes feeding the same outcome. If behavior is supported by many similar channels, blocking only one or a few channels may produce little visible effect.

concept C │ ├─ feature pipe 1 ─┐ ├─ feature pipe 2 ─┤ ├─ feature pipe 3 ─┤→ downstream behavior ├─ ... ─┤ └─ feature pipe n ─┘

This naturally points to richer geometries of representation: distances, neighborhoods, clusters, and trajectories may encode task-relevant structure beyond individual linear directions.

Feature graphs and subcircuits

Extending the analogy, we can imagine the model as a large computational graph where nodes correspond to features and edges correspond to transformations or interactions among them.

If only a small subset of features activates for a given input slice, then different inputs may recruit different subgraphs. In principle, this suggests a decomposition into partially specialized subcircuits, each solving a distinct computational role.

Some subcircuits may resemble known motifs such as induction circuits discussed in Anthropic's induction head work. A long-term interpretability goal is to identify many such circuits and understand how they compose into full-model behavior.

Interpretability implication. Searching for single "the feature" explanations may be less productive than mapping families of related features and their interaction topology.

Conclusion

Features are best viewed as optimization-shaped statistics: initially unstructured, later tuned into useful computational ingredients. Linear structure is often real and important, but likely incomplete as a full account of representation. In large models, concepts appear to be distributed, relational, and circuit-level rather than atomic.
